Issue One: Payment ≠ Guilt
On the Trap That Is Rule 12 · Settled Since McKevitt v Common Sense
Magistrate Seán submitted that payment of the fine confirms guilt. His Honour Doc AJ rejects this without hesitation. ↗ ¶5–6 Full Decision
The Rule 12 Problem
Rule 12 compels payment before the next match-day or a further $5 fine accrues. Both Simo and Doc AJ paid fines under this provision. Payment under compulsion of penalty accrual is not voluntary. It is not an admission. It is a survival strategy.
Acceptance of Seán's submission would immunise every wrongly-issued fine from challenge. That cannot be right. That is a trap. That is, if the Court may say so, a bit fkn suss.
Controlling Authority
McKevitt v Common Sense [2021] MFCSC 12 — appeal dismissed, costs awarded, dignity lost. Not to be confused with Macca, who is the goalkeeper, not the fines master, and who was not a party to that proceeding.
Finding
Payment under Rule 12 duress ≠ admission. Right of appeal preserved. Submission rejected. Both Simo's and Doc's payments are noted as implied compliance with a rule now rendered express.

The Second Limb: "Gear"
What Rule 2 Actually Covers · And What It Doesn't · ¶12–14
Even if the trainers had been genuinely missing, Rule 2 would still not apply. The Schedule must be read as a coherent whole. ↗ ¶13
"Gear" = Football Gear
In Scope — ¶13
Boots. Shin pads. Socks. Jersey. Shorts. Items required to participate. See also: the General, Week 1, no shin pads, full kit bag incident.
Mischief Rule 2 Addresses
Turning up unable to play because you forgot your kit, you forgetful nonce. Nothing more. Nothing less.
Trainers ≠ Gear
Out of Scope — ¶13
Simo's trainers. Personal civilian footwear. Post-match. Not footballing items. Not required to play. Nice ones, apparently.

Simo was in possession of all required football gear during the match. He was missing nothing. He was, in fact, present and on time — notable given he thought kick-off was at 3PM.
